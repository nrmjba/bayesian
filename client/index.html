the bad news

You are a terrible guesser.

I'm also a terrible guesser. As are most people, with the exception of some well-trained meterologists and professional gamblers.

When I say 'guessing,' I'm referring to a properly calibrated probability estimate. If one were to guess the gross domestic earnings of The Goonies, the answer would probably be wrong, because this tests specific domain knowledge. Rephrasing the question as:

"What are your high and low estimates for the gross domestic earnings of the The Goonies? What interval estimate of confidence would give this range?"
The answer might be:
"Between $100,000 to $100,000,000, inclusive, with 90% confidence."
Given enough data points (say, the 100 top grossing films of 1980-1989) in a sufficiently ideal world, the estimated confidence interval would match the observed accuracy. In other words, the range estimates given with 90% confidence should – on average and with minimal variance – be correct 90% of the time. This changes the exercise from a measurement of trivia knowledge to a measurement of ability to gauge uncertainty. However, only about 70% [ citation needed ] of estimated ranges with a 90% confidence interval will contain the actual value, not because of a lack of public knowledge in 20th century film economics, but because humans habitually display overconfident estimates. What we know is about 25-45 percent less than what we think we know [ citation needed ].

The exact answer is $61,389,680.

cognitive bugs

There exists much more research investigating the limits and prejudgices of probability estimages than in their potential causes. The theories below are well-reasoned, but lack empirical evidence. (Note: while these theories are well thought-out and based on the attributed research, there is little to know empirical tests establishing causation to the 'uncalibration' of probability estimates).

Cognitive biases that act against our correct estimation of prior probablity:

  • anchoring
  • the pattern-seeking habits of human perception create the illusion of signal where there is only noisy data
  • Prospect theory: small probabilities are overweighted, especially when attached to high-consequence events (p100, Scott Plous)
  • we seek to reduce risk/uncertainty, and will pay an economic premium for it (p100, Plous)
  • "missing, noisy or imprecise variables." Ambguity makes us uncomfortable.
  • Our educational training emphasizes algebra and calculus as the end goals, not probability and statistics
These biases are to estimation as geometrical-optical illusions [image] are to psychometrics. A simple change of the problem context causes a predicatable change in the perceived reality. In general, humans have a very troubled relationship with uncertainty. We don't understand it instintually, and a large part of current information science is devoted to ways of accurately communicating it.

you should care

Remember the last time you said you would be someplace, only there was traffic, you hit every red-light and got a flat tire?

Or that time your project estimate was off by an order-of-magnitude?

Or when that investment turned out so badly it scared you back into bonds and CD's for next ten years?

Even if you don't live in a region with legalized gambling or work in a forecasting profession, your inability to estimate hurts you.

mensa mea bona est

Overconfident guessing follows a predictable pattern. Overconfidence is usual for difficult assessments (although slightly less for T/F tests; Hubbard p64). Underconfidence for easy ones. Calibration fixes this.

  • Rationalize answers with pros and cons (Hubbard p64)
  • Range estimates to reduce the anchoring effect of a point estimate. Particularly, working towards a narrow range for an absurdly large range.
  • Confidence intervals

the n-step method

  • Get comfortable with uncertainty, particularly the idea of giving uncertain unaswers
  • Practice gauging your uncertainty
  • Think probablistically!
